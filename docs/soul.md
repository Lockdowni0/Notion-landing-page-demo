# Notion 2013 Landing Page — Soul

## 1. Purpose

This landing page is an interactive revival of the March 2013 Notion business plan. It should not imitate Notion's current website and should not feel like a retrospective museum exhibit. It should feel as if the original 2013 idea has become a living, usable web experience.

The page must communicate one central belief:

> Software should be shaped by the people who use it.

The visitor should leave understanding that Notion is not merely another productivity application. It is a visual environment where non-programmers can turn information into software.

## 2. Core Brand Idea

**Democratize Software.**

Bring the creative power of computing to people who do not write code. Start with an empty page, give information structure, assemble reusable parts, and arrive at a working tool.

The product promise is transformation:

```text
page -> structure -> tool -> shared software
```

Every section, interaction, and line of copy must reinforce this transformation.

## 3. Reciprocal Design Law

The landing page must enact the quotation rather than merely display it:

> We shape our tools, and thereafter our tools will shape us.

The canonical design law is:

> Every action leaves a structure. Every structure changes what happens next.

The experience therefore has two connected movements:

### We shape our tools

The visitor chooses, adds, edits, arranges, names, and connects. Their actions create a visible structure rather than triggering a pre-scripted spectacle.

### Our tools shape us

The structure the visitor created changes the rest of the experience. Their chosen project name, fields, view, and status model should reappear in later sections, alter how information is organised, and demonstrate how a tool creates a shared way of working.

This reciprocity must remain legible and restrained. The page should adapt in meaningful, bounded ways; it must not become an unpredictable personalised theme generator.

## 4. Visual Thesis

An optimistic 2013 vision of future software, expressed through the quiet material language of paper, editorial typography, technical diagrams, and hand-drawn tools.

The desired emotional qualities are:

- thoughtful, not corporate;
- visionary, not futuristic theatre;
- handmade, not rustic;
- technical, not intimidating;
- sparse, not empty;
- confident, not loud;
- historical in texture, contemporary in usability.

The page should feel like a manifesto that gradually becomes an editor.

## 5. The Central Metaphor

The hand-drawn stone tool from the original cover is the primary visual anchor.

It represents three ideas:

1. Humans have always shaped tools.
2. Tools reshape human behaviour.
3. Notion is a new kind of foundational tool: soft, modular, and personal.

The stone must not be used as decorative clip art. Its appearance should begin the product story. Through motion and composition, its structure should lead toward blocks, fields, and software modules. The transformation is continuous: an irregular object shaped by a person becomes a modular tool that can later organise human action.

## 6. Narrative Arc

The page follows a six-part argument:

1. **Belief** — We shape our tools, and our tools shape us.
2. **Problem** — Software has become a collection of rigid, disconnected applications.
3. **System** — Notion unifies information and software through a layered visual environment.
4. **Proof** — The visitor builds a small working tool inside the product demo.
5. **Possibility** — Reusable modules can serve many kinds of software.
6. **Invitation** — Anyone can begin shaping their own tools.

The emotional movement is:

```text
curiosity -> recognition -> clarity -> agency -> possibility -> action
```

The structural movement is:

```text
visitor shapes interface -> interface becomes a tool -> tool reshapes later content and collaboration
```

## 7. Voice and Copy

The voice is direct, intelligent, warm, and compact. It assumes the visitor can understand an ambitious idea without excessive explanation.

### Copy principles

- Prefer short declarative sentences.
- Lead with the product idea, not design commentary.
- Use plain words: page, information, structure, tool, software, build, shape.
- Let one sentence carry one idea.
- Keep body copy to one or two short sentences per section.
- Use the original plan's language when it remains clear and relevant.
- Use italics sparingly for philosophical or conceptual emphasis.
- Product UI labels must be functional and unambiguous.

### Canonical phrases

- `Democratize Software.`
- `A centralized home for your information & software.`
- `Start with a page. Shape it into a tool.`
- `Build once. Serve many.`
- `We shape our tools, and thereafter our tools will shape us.`

### Avoid

- generic SaaS claims such as “supercharge productivity”;
- contemporary AI language;
- exaggerated words such as revolutionary, magical, seamless, or game-changing;
- dense investor language, market sizing, fundraising, or growth metrics;
- explaining the visual design inside the page copy;
- presenting the concept as a nostalgic recreation.

## 8. Product Demo Soul

The demo is the proof of the thesis, not a decorative screenshot.

The visitor must experience a legible transformation:

1. Begin with an empty page.
2. Add ordinary content.
3. Give that content fields and relationships.
4. Change its view and behaviour.
5. Preview it as working software.

The demo should make the visitor feel capable. It must be understandable without a tutorial video, forgiving of experimentation, and resettable at any time.

Its project-management scenario is only an example. The deeper message is that the same building blocks could become many different tools.

The demo must capture a small, explicit set of visitor decisions and carry them forward. At minimum:

- the project name;
- the selected view, such as Table or Board;
- the fields or workflow states the visitor created.

After preview, the page must acknowledge that the visitor has made something. Later diagrams should use the visitor's structure to show how software influences coordination, attention, and next actions.

## 9. Restraint

Restraint is a brand behaviour, not just a visual preference.

- One dominant idea per section.
- One primary action per moment.
- One persistent accent colour.
- No visual element without narrative or functional purpose.
- No feature inventory when a transformation can explain the product better.
- No motion that does not communicate assembly, change, hierarchy, or feedback.
- No adaptation that is merely cosmetic or makes the page harder to understand.

If removing an element improves comprehension without weakening the concept, remove it.

## 10. Historical Fidelity

The source of truth is the attached `Notion_2013.pdf`.

The landing page should preserve:

- coral-red emphasis;
- generous white space;
- black, grey, and pale-tint diagrams;
- humanist sans-serif typography;
- italic serif accents;
- square software blocks;
- simple people symbols, arrows, and structural diagrams;
- a calm presentation rhythm with one argument per frame.

The page should not copy:

- outdated application screenshots as primary visuals;
- investor-only market numbers;
- the Series A roadmap;
- obsolete technical claims;
- every slide as a separate website section.

Historical fidelity means preserving the original idea and visual grammar, not reproducing every slide literally.

## 11. Non-Negotiable Test

Before accepting any major design or implementation decision, ask:

1. Does this help the visitor understand that information can become software?
2. Does it feel rooted in the 2013 document rather than today's Notion brand?
3. Does it give the visitor agency instead of merely describing features?
4. Is it quiet enough for the central idea to remain dominant?
5. Does an action taken by the visitor leave a meaningful trace that affects what happens next?

If the answer to any of these is no, revise the decision.
